Federated Causal Discovery via Graph Reinforcement Learning on Overlapping Variable SetsIntroduction to the Thematic Architecture and Theoretical FoundationsThe identification of causal structures from observational data is a foundational challenge across empirical sciences, representing the critical transition from purely associative machine learning toward robust, interventional reasoning. Traditional causal discovery paradigms are generally divided into constraint-based algorithms, which rely on conditional independence tests to prune fully connected graphs, and score-based algorithms, which utilize local heuristics to navigate the combinatorial space of Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) to maximize a predefined statistical metric. However, these traditional methodologies frequently encounter severe limitations when applied to high-dimensional datasets or when operating under finite-sample constraints, leading to unstable graph estimations and high computational overhead.In recent years, Graph Reinforcement Learning (GRL) has emerged as a highly effective, alternative optimization paradigm for causal discovery. By framing the derivation of a causal graph as a sequential decision-making process, GRL agents iteratively refine an adjacency matrix, learning a search policy that bypasses the limitations of greedy local heuristics. Concurrently, the proliferation of decentralized data silos in fields such as healthcare and finance has necessitated the development of federated causal discovery frameworks. In real-world distributed environments, isolated clients frequently observe overlapping but non-identical variable sets. Learning a globally consistent causal structure under these privacy-preserving constraints introduces the acute risk of spurious dependencies, as variables completely unobserved by one client act as latent confounders within its local estimation.The analysis presented herein constructs an exhaustive theoretical and algorithmic framework tailored for a master's thesis project designed to be executed within an eight-week timeframe. The objective is to facilitate the learning of a global causal graph across distributed agents observing overlapping variable spaces using Graph Reinforcement Learning. The system architecture models a ten-variable linear Gaussian Structural Causal Model (SCM), where the global variable set $\mathcal{V} = \{V_1, V_2, \dots, V_{10}\}$ is partitioned such that Agent 1 observes the subset $\mathcal{V}_1 = \{V_1, \dots, V_6\}$, and Agent 2 observes the subset $\mathcal{V}_2 = \{V_5, \dots, V_{10}\}$. The overlapping nodes, $\mathcal{O} = \{V_5, V_6\}$, serve as the critical informational bridge between the two localized models.By formulating specific mathematical communication protocols—ranging from soft penalty consensus incorporated directly into the RL reward function to hard action masking derived from deterministic orientation rules—the agents leverage localized Graph Reinforcement Learning to iteratively construct their structural hypotheses. Crucially, the exchange of topological beliefs regarding the shared variables enables the agents to break local Markov Equivalence Classes (MECs), mitigating the confounding effects of relative latent variables and successfully reconstructing the true global DAG.Baseline Literature ReviewThe intersection of reinforcement learning, causal structure learning, and federated systems requires the synthesis of highly specialized, state-of-the-art literature. The following peer-reviewed works represent the proven baseline methods and the theoretical bedrock necessary to implement pragmatic GRL-based causal search within strict computational constraints. The selection explicitly prioritizes stable, mathematically grounded frameworks over highly theoretical continuous optimization architectures that are notoriously difficult to tune within short academic deadlines.1. Causal Discovery with Reinforcement LearningAuthors: Shengyu Zhu, Ignavier Ng, Zhitang Chen (2020)
Core Contributions: This seminal paper introduced the conceptual framework of treating causal discovery as a reinforcement learning optimization problem, shifting the paradigm away from traditional local heuristics. The authors developed an actor-critic encoder-decoder architecture that ingests observational data and outputs a sequence representing a directed graph adjacency matrix. Crucially, rather than explicitly restricting the RL action space to strict DAGs—which is computationally prohibitive to calculate continuously—the framework utilizes a smooth acyclicity penalty derived from the NOTEARS formulation, integrating it directly into the reward function. By utilizing the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) as the core scoring mechanism, the agent learns a stochastic search strategy that effectively navigates the combinatorial space. This paper establishes the fundamental reward engineering equations required for single-agent graph generation and proves that RL agents can discover optimally scoring graphs that traditional methods miss.2. Ordering-Based Causal Discovery with Reinforcement Learning (CORL)Authors: Xiaoqiang Wang, Yali Du, Shengyu Zhu, Liangjun Ke, Zhitang Chen, Jianye Hao, Jun Wang (2021)
Core Contributions: Expanding upon the foundational work of Zhu et al., the CORL framework addresses the inherent scalability limits of searching through the entire directed graph space. The authors recognized that the continuous acyclicity penalty struggles as the number of variables increases, leading to instability. By shifting the search space from the space of all possible graphs (which scales super-exponentially) to the space of variable orderings or permutations (which scales factorially), CORL formulates the RL task as a multi-step ordering-search Markov Decision Process. The RL agent incrementally selects variables to construct a valid topological ordering. Once the ordering is established, a deterministic variable selection process—such as standard regression with sparsity constraints—identifies the active parent-child relationships. For tight computational deadlines, projecting the graph search into an ordering search provides a highly pragmatic, stable baseline that ensures acyclicity by mathematical definition rather than relying on soft penalties.3. Competition is the key: A Game Theoretic Causal Discovery ApproachAuthors: (Preprint / DDQN-CD, 2025)
Core Contributions: This paper addresses the finite-sample inconsistencies of continuous and ordering-based heuristic methods by introducing a Double Deep Q-Network (DDQN) agent that operates in a game-theoretic framework against a classical baseline opponent, such as the Greedy Equivalence Search (GES). Rather than generating graphs from scratch, the agent's action space consists of localized discrete edge edits—specifically Add, Remove, and Reverse operations—applied to an initial "warm-start" graph provided by the opponent. Action masking is strictly applied at every step to guarantee adherence to acyclicity and edge budget constraints. This methodology is highly relevant for decentralized graph building, as the discrete local edit action space allows for direct translation into federated action masking constraints, providing a robust architecture for multi-agent synchronization.4. Federated Causal Structure Learning with Non-identical Variable Sets (FedCDnv)Authors: (Preprint / Wang et al., 2025)
Core Contributions: Directly addressing the exact structural overlap problem central to this thesis, FedCDnv establishes the theoretical foundation for integrating non-identical variable sets across distributed clients. The authors categorize latent variables into absolute latents (unobserved by all) and relative latents (observed by some clients but not others). They demonstrate mathematically how non-overlapping variable pairs create spurious dependencies in local models due to these relative latents acting as unobserved confounders. The paper proposes a multi-level priority selection strategy that evaluates the stability of local causal relations across varying significance levels of conditional independence tests. By selectively aggregating "good" and "correct" structural linkages on a central server, this framework explains how overlapping variables transmit sufficient conditional independence information to constrain and correct the broader global graph.5. Regret-Based Federated Causal Discovery with Unknown Interventions (I-PERI)Authors: Federico Baldo, Charles K. Assaad (2025)
Core Contributions: Operating under the realistic assumption that different clients experience heterogeneous, unknown interventions or differing data-generating contexts, I-PERI utilizes a regret-minimization optimization loop to recover an aggregate Completed Partially Directed Acyclic Graph (CPDAG). The methodology introduces the $\Phi$-Markov Equivalence Class, mathematically proving that structural differences in local graphs—induced by environmental shifts or partial observability—actually provide critical orienting information that drastically reduces the size of the MEC. To accurately reconstruct the data-generating process within the simulation phase of the thesis, the global environment must be mathematically formalized. Let the global environment be defined by a set of continuous random variables $\mathcal{V} = \{V_1, V_2, \dots, V_{10}\}$. The data-generating process assumes an additive noise formulation governed by the true global DAG $G^*$:$$V_j = f_j(\text{Pa}_{G^*}(V_j)) + \epsilon_j \quad \forall j \in \{1, \dots, 10\}$$
where $\text{Pa}_{G^*}(V_j)$ denotes the parent set of variable $V_j$ in the true global DAG, $f_j$ represents the linear functional relationships defined by a weight matrix $W$, and $\epsilon_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ represents the mutually independent Gaussian noise terms applied to each node. The assumption of independent noise is critical, as it guarantees that any statistical dependence observed between variables is a direct result of the causal structure rather than correlated exogenous interference.The federated constraint separates this global domain into two partial observation sets. Agent 1 observes the subset $\mathcal{V}_1 = \{V_1, V_2, V_3, V_4, V_5, V_6\}$, while Agent 2 observes the subset $\mathcal{V}_2 = \{V_5, V_6, V_7, V_8, V_9, V_{10}\}$. The intersection of these domains forms the overlap set $\mathcal{O} = \mathcal{V}_1 \cap \mathcal{V}_2 = \{V_5, V_6\}$.This partial observability introduces the phenomenon of relative latent variables. From the perspective of Agent 2, the variables $\{V_1, V_2, V_3, V_4\}$ are entirely unobserved. If the true global DAG dictates a causal chain such as $V_4 \rightarrow V_5 \rightarrow V_7$, Agent 2 can observe the correlation between $V_5$ and $V_7$. However, if a more complex structure exists, such as $V_4 \rightarrow V_5$ and $V_4 \rightarrow V_6$, the unobserved variable $V_4$ acts as a hidden common cause (a relative latent confounder) for $V_5$ and $V_6$ from Agent 2's perspective. Without communication from Agent 1, Agent 2 will detect a strong statistical dependence between $V_5$ and $V_6$ and may falsely conclude a direct causal link exists (either $V_5 \rightarrow V_6$ or $V_6 \rightarrow V_5$), introducing a spurious dependency into its local graph. Resolving this exact confounding is the primary objective of the multi-agent GRL framework.Markov Decision Process Formulation for Graph SearchTo facilitate discovery without the transmission of raw observational data, both agents must employ localized Graph Reinforcement Learning. While the continuous action space utilized by Zhu et al. is highly expressive, it relies on complex Augmented Lagrangian optimization that scales poorly and frequently suffers from hyperparameter sensitivity. Therefore, to ensure the approach remains pragmatic and computationally feasible for a tight deadline, the system architecture must model the graph search as a localized discrete Markov Decision Process (MDP) akin to the DDQN-CD framework.State Space DefinitionThe state $s_t^{(i)}$ for agent $i \in \{1, 2\}$ at step $t$ is the agent's current local graph, represented by a binary adjacency matrix $A^{(i)}_t \in \{0, 1\}^{p_i \times p_i}$, where $p_i = 6$ represents the number of variables accessible to that specific agent. A matrix value of $A_{j,k} = 1$ indicates the presence of a directed edge from variable $V_j$ to variable $V_k$. The initial state $s_0^{(i)}$ can be formulated as a completely empty graph, allowing the agent to build the structure from scratch, or as a "warm-start" graph generated by a rapid classical algorithm such as the PC algorithm or Greedy Equivalence Search executed on the local data to accelerate convergence.Action Space FormulationThe action space $\mathcal{A}^{(i)}$ consists of deterministic, localized structural edits on ordered node pairs $(j, k)$ where $j \neq k$. At any given state, the reinforcement learning agent may select one of three discrete actions for a specified edge within its local adjacency matrix:$\text{ADD}(j \rightarrow k)$: Inserts a directed edge by updating $A_{j,k} = 1$.$\text{REMOVE}(j \rightarrow k)$: Deletes an existing directed edge by updating $A_{j,k} = 0$.$\text{REVERSE}(j \rightarrow k)$: Inverts the causal direction by simultaneously updating $A_{j,k} = 0$ and $A_{k,j} = 1$.Invalid Action Masking and Acyclicity EnforcementTo constrain the search space strictly to valid Directed Acyclic Graphs and circumvent the need for complex, unstable continuous penalties, the architecture relies on strict Action Masking at the MDP transition level. Before the RL agent's policy network outputs an action distribution for the next step, a dynamic binary mask $m_t$ is computed for all possible actions.For any proposed addition or reversal action, the environment utilizes a fast graph traversal algorithm, such as Depth-First Search (DFS) or Kahn's algorithm for topological sorting, to simulate the resulting graph. If the simulated action induces a cyclic dependency (e.g., $V_1 \rightarrow V_2 \rightarrow V_3 \rightarrow V_1$), the corresponding action is masked out ($m_{t,a} = 0$). During action selection, the agent is mathematically forced to sample only from the subset of actions where $m_{t,a} = 1$, ensuring that the agent's trajectory strictly navigates the manifold of valid DAGs without requiring the neural network to implicitly learn the acyclicity constraint.Reward Function Design for the Single AgentIn the absence of inter-agent communication, each agent must independently evaluate the validity of its proposed graph modifications against its local observational data distribution. This evaluation is achieved through a localized reward function guided by a recognized scoring metric, predominantly the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), which naturally balances statistical model fit against structural complexity to prevent the RL agent from generating overly dense, overfitted graphs.Mathematical Formulation of the BIC ScoreFor continuous, linear-Gaussian data, the likelihood of a proposed local DAG $G^{(i)}$ factorizes over the individual variables according to the causal Markov condition, which states that a variable is conditionally independent of its non-descendants given its direct parents in the causal graph. The BIC score for the local graph is mathematically defined as:
$$S_{BIC}(G^{(i)}) = \sum_{v \in \mathcal{V}_i} \left[ - 2 \ln \hat{L}(V_v | \text{Pa}(V_v); \theta_v) + |\text{Pa}(V_v)| \ln m_i \right]$$Where:$\hat{L}(V_v | \text{Pa}(V_v); \theta_v)$ represents the maximized likelihood of the variable given its current proposed parents in the adjacency matrix, parameterized by the regression coefficients $\theta_v$.$|\text{Pa}(V_v)|$ represents the number of parameters (the number of incoming edges or parents) for variable $V_v$, establishing a strict sparsity penalty.$m_i$ denotes the total number of observational samples available in the client's local dataset.Because optimization in reinforcement learning typically aims to maximize a reward signal, and a lower BIC score indicates a superior statistical model, the raw BIC value must be inverted or properly scaled before being fed back to the agent.Step-wise Reward StructuringWhile some RL causal discovery frameworks utilize an episodic generation structure—where the agent sequentially builds an entire graph before receiving a single terminal reward—the discrete local-edit MDP defined in the previous section benefits immensely from dense, step-wise rewards.When the agent executes an action $a_t$ transitioning the graph from $A_t$ to $A_{t+1}$, the baseline reward function is defined as the differential delta of the BIC score:
$$r_{local}(A_t, a_t, A_{t+1}) = \frac{S_{BIC}(A_t) - S_{BIC}(A_{t+1})}{p_i} - \lambda_{sparse} \| A_{t+1} \|_0 - c_{step}$$
Within this formulation, $p_i$ normalizes the score change over the number of variables. The term $\lambda_{sparse} \| A_{t+1} \|_0$ introduces an optional $L_0$ regularization norm representing the total count of active edges, serving as an additional hyperparameter to govern graph sparsity. The term $c_{step}$ is a minor negative constant cost applied to each step. This step cost is critical in reinforcement learning; without it, the agent might discover a sequence of actions (e.g., endlessly adding and removing the same edge) that yields zero net score change but allows the episode to continue indefinitely. The step cost incentivizes the agent to reach a locally optimal graph and terminate the episode efficiently. If an agent attempts an invalid action that bypasses the mask, it is heavily penalized with a negative scalar reward $-\delta$, and the state remains unchanged ($A_{t+1} = A_t$).Federated Communication Protocols on Overlapping VariablesThe primary architectural challenge of the thesis arises from the constraint that the raw data instances for the full set of variables $V_1 \dots V_{10}$ cannot be pooled centrally. Agent 1 and Agent 2 must independently search for the optimal local DAGs. However, without communication, edges involving the overlapping nodes $\mathcal{O} = \{V_5, V_6\}$ will inevitably suffer from unidentifiability and relative latent confounding.Specifically, constraint-based and score-based causal discovery algorithms generally output a Markov Equivalence Class (MEC), represented by a Completed Partially Directed Acyclic Graph (CPDAG), rather than a unique, fully oriented DAG. The MEC contains all DAGs that encode the exact same set of conditional independencies. Within a CPDAG, some edges are strictly oriented because they form unique v-structures (unshielded colliders), while other edges remain undirected because multiple orientations fit the data equally well.If Agent 1 observes a v-structure such as $V_4 \rightarrow V_5 \leftarrow V_6$, it can unambiguously orient the edge between $V_5$ and $V_6$ because $V_4$ and $V_6$ are marginally independent but become dependent when conditioning on $V_5$. However, Agent 2 does not observe $V_4$. Left in isolation, Agent 2 will find $V_5$ and $V_6$ to be statistically dependent but may be entirely unable to orient the edge, or worse, may orient it incorrectly due to local spurious correlations induced by the relative latent variable $V_4$.To bridge this gap and break the local MECs, the agents must communicate their evolving beliefs concerning the subgraph spanning the overlapping variables. The two subsequent mathematical protocols dictate how this structural information transfer can modulate the local Graph RL optimization process to guarantee global consistency.Protocol A: Soft Consensus via Penalty AugmentationIn the Soft Consensus approach, the federated system does not explicitly force the agents to adopt identical structures. Instead, the agents periodically exchange their estimated confidence regarding the overlapping variables, and the system incorporates a continuous structural divergence penalty directly into the RL reward functions.At regular synchronization intervals (e.g., every $K$ training episodes), Agent 1 and Agent 2 upload their current best-estimate adjacency submatrices covering the overlap, denoted as $A_1^{\mathcal{O}}$ and $A_2^{\mathcal{O}} \in \{0, 1\}^{2 \times 2}$, to a central parameter server. The server computes a target consensus representation $\bar{A}^{\mathcal{O}}$, which can be a simple mathematical average of the adjacency matrices or a weighted average determined by the Q-value confidence of the respective agents.During the subsequent training phase, the reward function for Agent $i$ is augmented with a consensus penalty $P_{consensus}$:$$r_{augmented}(A_t, a_t, A_{t+1}) = r_{local}(A_t, a_t, A_{t+1}) - \gamma \| A_{t+1}^{\mathcal{O}} - \bar{A}^{\mathcal{O}} \|_F^2$$
Where $\gamma$ is a scalar hyperparameter that dictates the strictness of the consensus, and $\| \cdot \|_F^2$ is the squared Frobenius norm measuring the mathematical distance between the agent's current overlap state and the global consensus.Mechanism Analysis and Insights: Soft consensus is highly resistant to initial stochastic noise in the RL process. If Agent 2 confidently learns an incorrect orientation early in training due to a localized statistical anomaly, the soft penalty gently nudges Agent 1 toward that orientation. However, because it is only a penalty and not a hard rule, Agent 1 can resist the incorrect orientation if its local data strongly contradicts it (e.g., if Agent 1 possesses a robust v-structure that yields a massive BIC improvement). Over time, as both agents thoroughly explore the state space, the true causal drivers overcome the soft penalties, naturally leading to a globally optimal, aligned structure without creating artificial, irreconcilable conflicts that could cause the RL agents to diverge.Protocol B: Hard Constraints via Action Masking and Constraint PropagationIn contrast, the Hard Constraints protocol utilizes strict logical rules to lock in orientations that are provably identifiable in one agent's local space, transmitting them as unalterable environmental constraints to the other agent. This approach heavily relies on Meek's orientation rules and graphical d-separation properties.Assume Agent 1 discovers a v-structure $V_4 \rightarrow V_5 \leftarrow V_6$. Because $V_4$ and $V_6$ are independent marginally but dependent conditional on $V_5$, the orientation $V_5 \leftarrow V_6$ is deterministic and strictly identifiable within Agent 1's dataset. At the synchronization step, Agent 1 communicates this definitive directed edge constraint to Agent 2. Agent 2 then integrates this knowledge directly into its Graph RL action mask.Mathematically, let $\mathcal{C}^{\mathcal{O}}$ be the set of mandatory edge constraints established by strict causal identification rules on the overlap. For any action $a_t$ proposed by Agent 2 that involves editing the relationship between $V_5$ and $V_6$, the action mask is defined as:$$m_{t, a} = 
\begin{cases} 
0, & \text{if } a_t \text{ contradicts } \mathcal{C}^{\mathcal{O}} \\
0, & \text{if } a_t \text{ introduces a cyclic dependency given } \mathcal{C}^{\mathcal{O}} \\
1, & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}$$Furthermore, Agent 2 does not just passively accept this constraint; it propagates this hard constraint throughout its own local graph using Meek's Rules. Meek's Rules dictate how definitive orientations cascade through a partially directed graph to orient neighboring edges without introducing new v-structures or cycles. For example:Meek's Rule 1 (Avoid New V-Structures): If the hard constraint forces $V_5 \leftarrow V_6$, and Agent 2 identifies an undirected statistical dependency between $V_6$ and $V_7$ but no adjacency between $V_5$ and $V_7$, Agent 2 must orient the new edge as $V_6 \rightarrow V_7$. If it oriented the edge as $V_6 \leftarrow V_7$, it would create a new v-structure ($V_5 \leftarrow V_6 \leftarrow V_7$ creates a collider at $V_6$) that was not supported by the underlying conditional independence tests. Therefore, the RL environment's action mask automatically disables the $\text{ADD}(V_7 \rightarrow V_6)$ action.Meek's Rule 2 (Avoid Cycles): If the hard constraint forces an orientation that creates a directed chain (e.g., $V_5 \rightarrow V_6 \rightarrow V_7$), and there is an undirected edge between $V_5$ and $V_7$, the mask will automatically enforce the orientation $V_5 \rightarrow V_7$ to prevent a directed cycle.Mechanism Analysis and Insights: The Hard Constraints protocol vastly reduces the combinatorial search space, significantly accelerating RL convergence. Because Graph RL struggles with large action spaces, mathematically pruning the space using federated structural information acts as a powerful heuristic. However, this method is fundamentally brittle. Structural Hamming Distance (SHD)The Structural Hamming Distance is the paramount standard metric for evaluating causal graph discovery. Because discovery algorithms often output Markov Equivalence Classes, directly comparing two DAGs can be misleading (as multiple DAGs can represent the exact same probability distribution). SHD operates by converting both the estimated global DAG $\hat{G}_{global}$ and the ground-truth DAG $G^*$ into their respective CPDAG representations. It then counts the minimum total number of required edge insertions, deletions, or flips necessary to transform the estimated graph into the true graph.
Specifically, the distance aggregates:Missing Edges (False Negatives): A causal connection exists in $G^*$ but is absent in $\hat{G}_{global}$.Extra Edges (False Positives): A connection exists in $\hat{G}_{global}$ but has no basis in $G^*$.Reversed Edges: The structural connection exists in both graphs, but the causal orientation is inverted.
A lower SHD directly correlates to a more accurate graph retrieval. Evaluating SHD across iterations will demonstrate how effectively the federated communication protocols resolve edge ambiguities.2. True Positive Rate (TPR) and False Discovery Rate (FDR)To provide a more granular, statistical view of the edge detection capabilities:TPR (Recall): Measures the proportion of correctly identified directed edges out of the total directed edges present in the ground-truth DAG $G^*$. It indicates the algorithm's power to discover true causal links.FDR: Measures the proportion of falsely identified edges out of the total edges proposed by the RL agent. This metric is particularly critical in the federated setting; it will explicitly reveal if the partial observability constraints are causing the agents to hallucinate spurious edges due to relative latent variable confounding.3. Structural Intervention Distance (SID)While SHD measures topological differences, it fails to quantify the graph's utility for actual causal inference tasks, such as estimating Average Treatment Effects or generating counterfactuals. The Structural Intervention Distance (SID) evaluates the graphs based on their valid adjustment sets. SID counts the total number of node pairs $(X, Y)$ for which the estimated graph $\hat{G}_{global}$ yields an incorrect interventional distribution $P(Y | do(X))$.If an agent misorients an edge that forms part of a backdoor path, the graph will advocate for conditioning on the wrong variables, fundamentally corrupting any downstream causal analysis. The inclusion of SID is highly recommended for the thesis, as it proves that the federated GRL agent has not just approximated a network topology, but has successfully preserved the functional causal properties of the underlying Structural Causal Model.Execution Roadmap (8-Week Implementation Schedule)Designing, tuning, and evaluating a multi-agent reinforcement learning system for causal discovery is an intricate software engineering and mathematical challenge.
